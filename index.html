Sources of PAHs

What are Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons?

Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) are a class of persistent organic pollutants composed of two or more fused aromatic rings. Naphthalene, the simplest PAH with two benzene rings, serves as our model compound due to its high prevalence and environmental mobility.

Anthropogenic Sources

PAHs are primarily produced through incomplete combustion of fossil fuels and biomass. Major sources include:

  • Vehicle Exhaust: Traffic emissions contribute significantly to urban PAH pollution, with concentrations reaching 100-1000 ng/m³ in busy areas.
  • Coal Combustion: Residential heating and industrial processes release large quantities of PAHs, particularly in winter months.
  • Industrial Discharge: Petroleum refining, coking, and chemical manufacturing are major point sources.
  • Petroleum Leakage: Oil spills and underground storage tank leaks contaminate soil and groundwater.

Environmental Distribution

PAHs are widely distributed across environmental matrices with global patterns dominated by traffic and coal combustion sources. They persist in soil (half-life: months to years), water bodies, and atmospheric particulates, showing bioaccumulation potential in food chains.

NahAc-Naphthalene Molecular Docking

Using YASARA software, molecular docking simulations between the NahAc subunit and naphthalene substrate revealed a binding energy of -6.84 kcal/mol. The primary binding site is stabilized by hydrophobic interactions, validating our metabolic pathway design.

02

Hazards of PAHs

Ecotoxicity & Environmental Impact

PAHs exhibit strong bioaccumulation and ecotoxicity, causing severe damage to soil microbial communities and disrupting ecosystem functions. Studies show that PAH contamination can reduce soil microbial diversity by up to 40% and inhibit key enzymatic activities essential for nutrient cycling.

Human Health Risks

The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classifies several PAHs, including benzo[a]pyrene, as Group 1 carcinogens. These compounds exhibit:

  • Carcinogenic Effects: DNA adduct formation leading to mutations and cancer development.
  • Teratogenic Effects: Developmental abnormalities in exposed organisms.
  • Mutagenic Effects: Genetic damage across multiple generations.

Bioaccumulation & Persistence

Naphthalene, as a typical low-molecular-weight PAH, is highly mobile and easily exposed through inhalation, ingestion, and dermal contact. Its bioconcentration factor (BCF) ranges from 10-100, indicating moderate bioaccumulation potential. The compound's persistence in the environment poses long-term hidden risks to environmental safety and human health.

Regulatory Context

The WHO and EPA have established strict guidelines for PAH exposure. The EPA lists 16 priority PAHs for environmental monitoring, with naphthalene having a reference dose (RfD) of 0.02 mg/kg/day for oral exposure.

Wild-Type Growth Inhibition Test

Growth inhibition assays on LB agar plates containing 5.0 mM naphthalene showed significant wild-type microbial growth inhibition. This underscores the necessity of expressing exogenous degradation pathways to enable survival and remediation.

03

Our Solution

Limitations of Current Methods

Traditional PAH detection relies on large analytical instruments:

  • GC-MS & HPLC: Expensive equipment ($50,000-$200,000), time-consuming analysis (2-4 hours per sample), requires specialized facilities and trained personnel.
  • Conventional Remediation: Physical and chemical methods suffer from high costs ($100-$500 per ton of contaminated soil), low efficiency, and difficulty achieving in-situ treatment.

Our Engineered System

We designed an integrated Escherichia coli system combining detection and degradation capabilities:

Detection Module

Salicylic Acid Biosensor: Naphthalene is enzymatically converted to salicylic acid, which activates the NahR transcriptional regulator. This triggers expression of enhanced green fluorescent protein (eGFP), providing a visual readout with detection limits as low as 0.1 μM.

Degradation Module

NAH7 Plasmid-Based Pathway: The aerobic degradation pathway from Pseudomonas putida NAH7 plasmid enables complete mineralization of naphthalene through salicylic acid intermediates. Our engineered strain achieves 85-95% degradation efficiency within 48 hours under optimal conditions.

Key Advantages

  • High Sensitivity: Detection limits comparable to traditional methods but with real-time monitoring capability.
  • Low Cost: Estimated $5-$10 per sample, 10-20× cheaper than GC-MS analysis.
  • Field Deployment: Portable biosensor format suitable for on-site screening and in-situ bioremediation.
  • Environmental Adaptability: Engineered for robust performance across varying pH, temperature, and soil conditions.
  • Modular Design: Detection and degradation modules can be deployed independently or together based on application needs.

Our synthetic biology approach provides a safe, efficient, and scalable solution for environmental monitoring and governance of PAH pollution.

Promoter Expression Strength Tuning

Five promoters of varying strengths (J23100, J23104, J23106, J23114, J23119) were evaluated. Characterizing NahR-activated expression helped identify optimal transcription levels that minimize metabolic burden and cell toxicity.
